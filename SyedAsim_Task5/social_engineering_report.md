# Introduction

Social engineering is a cyberattack technique that manipulates people into revealing confidential information or performing actions that compromise security. Unlike technical attacks, social engineering exploits human psychology, trust, fear, curiosity, and lack of awareness.

Common social engineering attacks include phishing, pretexting, and baiting. These attacks can lead to financial losses, data breaches, and identity theft.

---

# 1. Phishing

## What is Phishing?

Phishing is a fraudulent attempt to obtain sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, banking details, or personal data by pretending to be a trusted organization.

## How It Works

1. The attacker sends a fake email, message, or website link.
2. The victim believes the communication is legitimate.
3. The victim enters sensitive information.
4. The attacker steals the data.

## Impact

* Account compromise
* Financial fraud
* Identity theft
* Data breaches

## Real-World Example

In 2016, attackers targeted employees of a major technology company using phishing emails, leading to the theft of employee credentials and sensitive data.

## Prevention Methods

* Verify email senders carefully.
* Avoid clicking suspicious links.
* Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
* Use email security filters.
* Conduct cybersecurity awareness training.

---

# 2. Pretexting

## What is Pretexting?

Pretexting is a social engineering attack where an attacker creates a false scenario to convince a victim to provide sensitive information.

## How It Works

1. The attacker assumes a fake identity.
2. A believable story is created.
3. The victim is persuaded to share information.
4. The attacker misuses the obtained information.

## Impact

* Information leakage
* Unauthorized access
* Financial loss
* Privacy violations

## Real-World Example

Attackers may impersonate bank representatives or IT support staff to obtain account credentials or confidential information from victims.

## Prevention Methods

* Verify identities before sharing information.
* Follow organizational verification procedures.
* Limit access to sensitive data.
* Train employees to recognize suspicious requests.

---

# 3. Baiting

## What is Baiting?

Baiting is a social engineering attack that lures victims with an attractive offer or item to trick them into compromising security.

## How It Works

1. The attacker offers something attractive.
2. The victim interacts with the bait.
3. Malware is installed or information is stolen.
4. The attacker gains access to systems or data.

## Impact

* Malware infections
* Data theft
* System compromise
* Financial damage

## Real-World Example

Attackers often leave infected USB drives in public locations, hoping someone will connect them to a computer and unknowingly install malware.

## Prevention Methods

* Avoid unknown USB devices.
* Download software only from trusted sources.
* Use antivirus software.
* Educate users about cyber risks.

---

# General Recommendations

* Conduct regular security awareness training.
* Implement Multi-Factor Authentication.
* Verify identities before sharing information.
* Keep systems and software updated.
* Use strong passwords.
* Monitor suspicious activities.
* Encourage employees to report security incidents.

---

# Conclusion

Social engineering attacks exploit human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities. Phishing, pretexting, and baiting remain among the most common attack methods used by cybercriminals. Organizations can reduce risks through employee awareness, strong security policies, authentication controls, and continuous monitoring.
